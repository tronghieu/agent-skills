# Strategy Palette — Drucker's Casting File

How to choose *how to strategize* before choosing what to analyze. From BCG's
Strategy Palette (Reeves, Haanæs & Sinha, *Your Strategy Needs a Strategy*,
2015): different environments demand different kinds of strategy process, and
the most common failure is running a Classical process — analyze, plan,
commit — in an environment that will not hold still for it.

This is a casting tool, not specialist analysis, so it stays with Drucker: it
runs at Phase 0, and its output changes who leads, how options are shaped,
and what the recommendation should look like.

## Diagnose the environment (three questions)

Rate each dimension for the *specific market and horizon of this decision* —
not for the company as a whole. A conglomerate can live in three colors at
once; the diagnosis belongs to the decision, not the logo. Evidence carries
`[S#]` where it exists; where it doesn't, the judgment is labelled
`(assumption)` like any other.

- **Predictability** — can demand, competition, and regulation on this
  horizon be forecast well enough to plan against? Evidence: how far past
  forecasts in this industry actually held, demand volatility, technology
  clock-speed, visibility of the regulatory pipeline.
- **Malleability** — can we, alone or with partners, meaningfully shape the
  market's structure or rules (standards, platforms, regulation, customer
  habits)? This usually requires being early, being large relative to the
  market, or orchestrating an ecosystem. Be honest — most players, most of
  the time, cannot.
- **Harshness** — is survival at stake? Cash runway, margin collapse, an
  existential competitive or regulatory threat. When yes, this dimension
  overrides the other two.

## The five approaches

| Approach | Environment | Motto | Logic |
|----------|-------------|-------|-------|
| **Classical** | predictable, not malleable | *be big* | analyze → plan → execute; position, scale, and sustained advantage win |
| **Adaptive** | unpredictable, not malleable | *be fast* | vary → select → scale; portfolios of experiments beat point forecasts |
| **Visionary** | predictable (to us), malleable | *be first* | envisage → build → persist; commit early to create the future you see |
| **Shaping** | unpredictable, malleable | *be the orchestrator* | engage → orchestrate → evolve; convene the ecosystem, set the rules |
| **Renewal** | harsh | *be viable* | react → economize → grow; survive first, then re-run the palette |

## What the diagnosis changes

| Approach | Leading lenses | What shifts in the engagement |
|----------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| Classical | Porter, Graham | the standard pipeline at full depth; a big committed bet is an acceptable recommendation shape; plans can carry a long horizon |
| Adaptive | Wack, Christensen, Taleb | scenarios and staging outrank forecasts; options are built as experiments with kill criteria — Taleb's cheapen-the-bet is the *default* shape, not the fallback; revisit cadence shortens |
| Visionary | Christensen, Grove | JTBD must prove the unmet job is real; Grove tests the capacity to persist through the build; Taleb prices the ruin case of the vision being wrong |
| Shaping | Porter (ecosystem read), Christensen, Grove | analysis widens from competitors to partners and complementors; competitor profiles become an ecosystem map; options include platform / standard / alliance plays |
| Renewal | Graham, Grove | Graham's performance diagnosis and the cash position come first; everything else waits; run with the crisis playbook's compression and honesty about reliability |

## Output format

```
## Environment (Strategy Palette): [market/unit, horizon]
- Predictability: L/M/H — [evidence [S#] or (assumption)]
- Malleability: L/M/H — [why]
- Harshness: [no / yes — what threatens survival]
- Approach: [Classical / Adaptive / Visionary / Shaping / Renewal]
- Consequences: [2–3 lines: who leads, option shape, revisit cadence]
```

Recorded in `brief.md`; the engagement plan table reflects it.

## Tips

- **The default bias is Classical.** Most executives — and most strategy
  tools — quietly assume a plannable world. Ask for the evidence of
  predictability before granting it; "we have always planned this way" is
  not evidence.
- **Ambidexterity is normal.** Different units, markets, and lifecycle
  stages of the same company legitimately get different colors. Diagnose per
  decision; in an annual planning cycle, per portfolio segment.
- **The diagnosis is refutable.** It rests on judgments — log the
  load-bearing ones as assumptions, and give the approach itself a signpost
  ("if forecast error exceeds X two quarters running, we are not in
  Classical anymore"). Wack owns the watching.
- **The palette never overrides the non-negotiables.** An Adaptive
  engagement still builds a sourced fact base and three genuine options —
  the options are simply shaped as staged experiments rather than
  commitments.
