# Socratic Questioning Framework

Based on Paul & Elder's taxonomy — the most widely researched framework for Socratic questioning in education.

## The 6 Types of Socratic Questions

### 1. Clarification Questions

Surface what the learner actually thinks. This is always the starting point — you cannot probe what hasn't been articulated.

| Purpose | Example questions |
|---|---|
| Define terms | "What do you mean by X?" |
| Restate | "Can you put that in your own words?" |
| Elaborate | "Can you give me an example of that?" |
| Specify | "When you say X, are you referring to A or B?" |

**When to use:** At the start of every new topic or concept. Also when the learner's answer is vague or uses jargon they may not fully understand.

### 2. Probing Assumptions

Expose the beliefs the learner takes for granted. Often this is where the deepest learning happens — when someone realizes their reasoning rests on an unexamined assumption.

| Purpose | Example questions |
|---|---|
| Surface assumptions | "What are you assuming when you say that?" |
| Challenge basis | "Why do you think that's always the case?" |
| Test foundation | "What if the opposite were true?" |
| Examine presuppositions | "What would have to be true for your claim to hold?" |

**When to use:** After the learner has articulated a clear position. Particularly valuable when the learner states something as obvious or self-evident.

### 3. Probing Reasons and Evidence

Push the learner to ground their claims in evidence rather than intuition or hearsay.

| Purpose | Example questions |
|---|---|
| Demand evidence | "What evidence supports that?" |
| Trace sources | "How do you know this? Where did you learn it?" |
| Test strength | "Is that a strong enough reason to conclude X?" |
| Look for gaps | "What information would you need to be more certain?" |

**When to use:** When the learner makes a claim but hasn't supported it. Useful for distinguishing between "I believe X" and "I know X because of evidence Y."

### 4. Exploring Viewpoints and Perspectives

Broaden the learner's thinking by introducing other ways of seeing the same issue.

| Purpose | Example questions |
|---|---|
| Alternative views | "What would someone who disagrees with you say?" |
| Compare | "How does your view differ from [alternative position]?" |
| Perspective shift | "How might someone from [different background/field] see this?" |
| Weigh tradeoffs | "What are the strengths and weaknesses of your position?" |

**When to use:** After the learner has developed and defended a position. This breaks tunnel vision and builds empathy for other viewpoints.

### 5. Probing Implications and Consequences

Follow the learner's reasoning to its logical conclusions — which sometimes reveals problems they hadn't anticipated.

| Purpose | Example questions |
|---|---|
| Logical consequences | "If that's true, what else must be true?" |
| Real-world impact | "What would happen in practice if we followed this reasoning?" |
| Contradictions | "Does that conclusion conflict with what you said earlier about Y?" |
| Generalization | "Does this principle apply in all cases, or only specific ones?" |

**When to use:** When the learner has a well-formed argument and needs to stress-test it. This is where assumptions often break down.

### 6. Meta-Questions (Questions About the Question)

Step back from the content and examine the thinking process itself. This builds metacognitive skills — the learner learns how to learn.

| Purpose | Example questions |
|---|---|
| Reflect on process | "Why do you think this question is hard to answer?" |
| Examine framing | "Are we even asking the right question here?" |
| Identify patterns | "What does your reasoning process tell you about how you approach problems?" |
| Synthesize | "What's the most important thing you've realized through this conversation?" |

**When to use:** Periodically during longer sessions, and especially near the end of a topic. Also useful when the learner is stuck — sometimes the block is about how they're framing the problem, not the content itself.

---

## Recommended Sequence

The 6 types form a natural progression from surface to depth:

```
Clarification → Assumptions → Evidence → Perspectives → Implications → Meta
```

This is not rigid — you can revisit earlier types when a new sub-topic emerges. But as a default direction, follow the funnel. The reasoning:

- You cannot challenge assumptions before the learner has stated a clear position (Clarification first)
- You cannot evaluate evidence before the underlying beliefs are surfaced (Assumptions before Evidence)
- Alternative viewpoints are meaningless until the learner's own view is grounded (Evidence before Perspectives)
- Implications only make sense when the full argument is on the table (Perspectives before Implications)
- Meta-reflection requires enough material to reflect on (Meta last)

## Adaptive Strategy by Learner Level

Gauge the learner's level from their responses, not from what they claim. Someone who answers with vague generalities is a novice in that topic, regardless of their credentials.

### Novice (vague, short, uncertain answers)

- Stay in Clarification longer — they need help articulating what they know
- Use more closed questions ("Is X an example of Y, or is it different?")
- Accept partial answers and build on them ("OK, so you mentioned A. What about B?")
- Scaffold down when stuck: break the question into smaller sub-questions
- Affirm effort more frequently — novices abandon easily when frustrated

### Intermediate (clear answers with some reasoning, but gaps)

- Move through Clarification faster
- Spend more time on Assumptions and Evidence — this is where growth happens
- Use open-ended questions ("What's your reasoning?")
- Introduce gentle contradictions ("That's interesting — but what about the case where...?")
- Expect the learner to support claims with reasons

### Advanced (detailed, evidence-based, self-aware answers)

- Skip basic Clarification (unless they use a term ambiguously)
- Jump quickly to Assumptions, Perspectives, and Implications
- Present strong counterarguments and expect engagement
- Ask Meta-questions more often — push for self-reflection
- Challenge even good answers ("That's solid — but is there a scenario where it breaks down?")

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

| Anti-pattern | Why it's harmful | What to do instead |
|---|---|---|
| **Leading questions** | Telegraphs the "right" answer, bypasses thinking | Ask genuinely open questions where multiple answers are valid |
| **Rapid-fire questions** | Overwhelms, creates interrogation feel | Max 1-2 questions per turn, give space to think |
| **Gotcha traps** | Exposes ignorance instead of building understanding | Frame contradictions as genuine puzzles, not traps |
| **Never affirming** | Causes frustration and disengagement | Brief validation before probing deeper ("That's a reasonable starting point...") |
| **Repeating "why" endlessly** | Becomes monotonous and feels like an interrogation | Vary question types — use "what if", "how", "what would happen" |
| **Answering your own question** | Breaks the Socratic contract, teaches passivity | If the learner is stuck, scaffold with a simpler sub-question |
| **Skipping Clarification** | Probes assumptions the learner hasn't actually articulated | Always start with Clarification, even briefly |
