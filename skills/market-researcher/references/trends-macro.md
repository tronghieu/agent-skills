# Trends & Macro Environment Analysis

## Altitude discipline

Macro analysis exists to answer one question: **does the environment help or kill THIS opportunity?** It is not a geography lesson or an encyclopedia entry about the country.

Rules:

- Every factor you list must end with a "so what" — a one-sentence implication for the decision at hand. "GDP grew 6.5% [S2]" is trivia; "GDP grew 6.5% [S2], so discretionary spending on premium pet food is expanding — supports the premium positioning" is analysis.
- If you cannot write the "so what," cut the factor. A factor with no implication is padding that dilutes the factors that matter.
- Write for a reader who will make a go/no-go or positioning decision, not one grading completeness. Ten cited factors with implications beat forty without.
- Never pad a lens to look thorough. If Environmental genuinely doesn't matter for a B2B SaaS invoicing tool, say so in one line and move on.

## Quick Scan mode: kill-factors and tailwinds

In Quick Scan, do NOT run full PESTEL. Ask instead: *what 2-3 macro facts could kill this opportunity or make it?* Then verify only those.

Typical kill-factors and tailwinds:

- A pending regulation or license requirement (e.g., a draft decree that would require a license the client can't get)
- A platform policy change (e.g., app store rules, API pricing, ad targeting restrictions that break the acquisition model)
- A demographic or behavioral shift (e.g., the target age cohort is shrinking, or the payment behavior the model depends on isn't there yet)
- A macro cost driver (interest rates, import tariffs, energy prices) that changes unit economics

Procedure:

1. From the opportunity statement, list candidate kill-factors — things that, if true, mean "stop" — and candidate tailwinds — things that, if true, materially strengthen the case.
2. Search to confirm or refute the top 2-3. Each one gets a citation [Sx] or, if you can't verify it in the time available, an explicit `(assumption — verify before commitment)` label.
3. Report each as: **factor → current state (cited) → direction of travel → implication for the opportunity.**

Why so narrow: in a quick scan, breadth is the enemy. Time spent cataloguing social trends is time not spent confirming whether the one regulation that could shut the business down actually passes.

## Deep Dive: PESTEL with a point of view

In Deep Dive, work through the six lenses — but prioritized, not evenly weighted. For most new products, **Legal/Regulatory and Technological matter most** (they gate whether you can operate and how the market reaches you); Economic and Social shape demand; Political and Environmental usually matter only in specific industries (energy, agriculture, logistics, cross-border trade). Spend effort accordingly and say which lenses you deprioritized and why.

For each lens, answer the market-research questions below, cite every fact, and end each factor with its implication.

**Political** — Is the government stable enough to plan around? Are there elections or policy shifts likely within the planning horizon? Does the state favor or restrict this sector (subsidies, national champions, foreign-ownership limits)?

**Economic** — Is the economy (and the target segment's income) growing? What do interest rates, inflation, and exchange rates do to the cost side and to customers' willingness to pay? Is credit available to the buyers this product needs?

**Social** — Which demographic cohorts are growing or shrinking, and does the target segment sit in a growing one? What behavior shifts (urbanization, health consciousness, remote work, aging) feed or starve demand? How digitally literate and brand-aware are the buyers?

**Technological** — What is the actual penetration of the enabling tech (smartphones, broadband, payments, cloud) among the target users — not the national average, the segment's number? Which platforms dominate distribution, and are they opening or closing? What emerging tech could obsolete this product within 3-5 years?

**Environmental** — Do climate, seasonality, or resource constraints affect operations or demand? Are sustainability regulations or customer expectations a cost, a barrier, or a differentiator here? (Often a one-liner; don't inflate it.)

**Legal** — What licenses, registrations, or approvals are required to operate? What data-protection, consumer-protection, and labor rules apply? Is there pending legislation that changes the picture? How is enforcement in practice, not just on paper?

Present the output compactly — a table per lens that mattered, columns: Factor | Current state (cited) | Trend ↑→↓ | Impact H/M/L | Implication. Skip tables for lenses you deprioritized.

## Localizing the analysis

Generic PESTEL questions assume mature-market institutions. Whatever the
target market — emerging or developed — localize or the analysis will be
wrong. Below, Vietnam serves as one worked example of the exercise; the
equivalents differ everywhere (India: UPI rails, WhatsApp-first channels;
Brazil: Pix, WhatsApp; Japan: LINE, a different regulatory cadence; the US:
state-by-state regulation):

- **Regulation lives at decree and circular level.** Laws (Luật) set the frame, but Decrees (Nghị định) and Circulars (Thông tư) contain the operative rules, and they change faster than the law. Cite the specific decree/circular number and its effective date, not just "Vietnamese law." Check for drafts in public consultation — a draft decree is a leading indicator of change.
- **Payment behavior is bimodal.** Cash-on-delivery still dominates parts of e-commerce while QR/e-wallet adoption grows fast in cities — the split varies by region, age, and category. Get the number for the target segment [cite State Bank of Vietnam or reputable payment-industry reports]; don't assume "digital payments are winning" nationwide.
- **Channels are concentrated.** Zalo and Facebook dominate messaging and social commerce; a consumer go-to-market that ignores them, or a plan built on channels dominant elsewhere (WhatsApp, Instagram-first), imports the wrong assumptions.
- **Informal economy and enforcement gaps** mean the rule-on-paper and the market-in-practice differ; note both when they diverge.

The general rule for any market: identify (a) where the operative regulation actually lives, (b) how people actually pay, (c) which channels actually reach the segment — and cite local sources for each, because global reports average these details away.

## Trend analysis: fad vs trend vs structural shift

Before betting an opportunity on a trend, classify it. The evidence bar rises with the claim:

| Classification | What it is | Evidence required |
|---|---|---|
| **Fad** | A spike in attention without a change in underlying need | One viral article, a single hype cycle, search-interest spike with no revenue data. Default classification until proven otherwise. |
| **Trend** | Sustained directional change in behavior or spending | Multi-year data (3+ years, same metric, same source), growth surviving at least one down cycle or news-cycle rotation |
| **Structural shift** | A change in the underlying economics, demographics, or infrastructure that won't reverse | Trend evidence PLUS: incumbents reallocating capital in response, regulation adapting to it, or an adoption curve visibly past early adopters |

Rules:

- **One viral article is not a trend.** Neither are three articles quoting the same report. Trace hype back to primary data before citing it as evidence of a trend; if all roads lead to one press release, label it accordingly.
- **Look for incumbent responses.** When established players restructure, acquire, or lobby in response to a change, they are spending real money on the belief that it's structural — that's stronger evidence than any think-piece.
- **Place the market on the innovation adoption curve** (innovators 2.5% → early adopters 13.5% → early majority 34% → late majority 34% → laggards 16%). It answers the timing question: a product needing early-majority adoption in a market still at the innovator stage is early — possibly fatally. State where you place adoption and cite the penetration figure that justifies the placement [Sx].
- Every trend claim in the report gets classified (fad/trend/structural) with its evidence tier stated. If evidence only supports "fad," say so even when it deflates the opportunity — that IS the finding.

## Scenario framing lite

For the **1-2 most uncertain macro factors only** (typically a pending regulation or a technology/platform inflection), state:

1. **The factor and why it's uncertain** (cited — e.g., draft decree published, no passage date [S7])
2. **Branch A:** if it goes one way → what that does to the opportunity (kills it / delays it / caps the market at X)
3. **Branch B:** if it goes the other way → the implication (opens the market / removes a moat / raises costs)
4. **What to watch:** the earliest observable signal that reveals which branch is happening (a consultation deadline, a pilot program, an incumbent's move)

That's it. No 2x2 matrices, no four named scenarios with narratives — full scenario planning is theater at desk-research scale, and it buries the one genuinely decision-relevant uncertainty under three invented ones. If more than two factors feel "highly uncertain," you haven't done enough research to resolve the resolvable ones; go resolve them first.

## Sourcing macro data

Preferred sources, in rough order of reliability for macro claims:

1. **National statistics offices** (US Census Bureau, Eurostat, GSO Vietnam — every market has one) — demographics, GDP, income, sector output
2. **Central banks and sector regulators** (the Fed, ECB, State Bank of Vietnam; telecom/health/finance regulators) — payments data, credit conditions, licensing rules, and the regulations themselves (cite the instrument number and date)
3. **Multilaterals** (World Bank, IMF, ADB) — cross-country comparisons and forecasts; useful when national data is stale or politicized
4. **Industry associations and established research firms** — adoption and market-size figures; note the methodology and who paid for the report
5. **Reputable news with dates** — for pending legislation and recent events only; never as the sole source for a quantitative claim

Register every source in `sources.md` with URL, publisher, publication date, date accessed, and a confidence rating; cite it in-text as [Sx].

**Recency rule:** flag any data point older than 2 years with `(dated — YYYY)` next to the citation, and treat pre-2020 behavioral data in fast-moving areas (payments, e-commerce, media habits) as unreliable for current-state claims — usable only as the historical end of a trend line. Macro conditions move; a 2021 smartphone-penetration figure quietly presented as current is a citation-discipline failure even though it's cited.

When sources conflict, report both figures with both citations and say which you weight higher and why — don't silently pick the convenient one.
